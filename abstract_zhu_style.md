## Abstract (Zhu et al. 2026 style)

The clinical usage of Traditional Chinese Medicines (TCMs) has gained increasing international attention for their distinct therapeutic effects, yet the safe application of herbs is constrained by their complex multi-ingredient, multi-target nature and the limited availability of annotated toxicity data. Traditional wet-lab pipelines for herb toxicity assessment are costly and time-consuming. Existing computational methods based on quantitative structure-activity relationships or network toxicology have shown promise, but often fail to effectively predict herb-level toxicity due to the multi-label nature of toxic effects and the scarcity of high-quality training data, and critically, they do not exploit the large volume of toxicity annotations accumulated for Western chemical drugs. To address these challenges, we propose a cross-domain molecular knowledge transfer framework for herb toxicity prediction. The framework first pretrains a Drug Tower encoder on 1,349 Western drugs annotated with 8 toxicity classes from the UniTox benchmark, and freezes the encoder to capture universal molecular toxicity patterns. Meanwhile, it trains a structurally identical CompoundToxModel on TCM compounds using herb-derived pseudo-labels, and aggregates per-compound predictions into a compound-level prior through a multi-head attention aggregator with five independent per-toxicity attention heads. Next, it projects compound fingerprints through the frozen Drug Tower encoder to obtain a drug-level prior, and adaptively fuses the dual-prior signals through a learned gating mechanism. It further introduces an Ensemble of Classifier Chains with asymmetric dual-pass conditioning (ECC-Adaptive+) and a non-symmetric molecular fingerprint augmentation (DropAdd) to refine prediction quality. Our framework achieves a macro-averaged AUC of 0.7821 on a curated dataset of 252 herbs with 5 toxicity labels, substantially outperforming the state-of-the-art HerbToxNet (AUC 0.6003) and other competitive baselines. Ablation studies across 16 configurations confirm that cross-domain drug knowledge transfer is the dominant performance driver, contributing +9.85 percentage points AUC. The multi-head attention aggregator additionally provides intrinsically interpretable compound-toxicity associations, enabling identification of key toxic ingredients without post-hoc explanation methods.

**Keywords**: Traditional Chinese Medicine; herb toxicity prediction; cross-domain transfer learning; ensemble of classifier chains; multi-label classification; molecular fingerprints
